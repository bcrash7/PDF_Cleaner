## THE BIG IDEA

## Some patients are paying up to $50,000 per year in fees for 'concierge medicine. ' Here's what's behind its rise.

By  Kara Miller Globe Correspondent, Updated November 15, 2024, 5:58 a.m.

Money can buy lots of stuff. But one of its greatest perks is the ability to make life more frictionless: A first-class airline ticket will expedite you through the airport and onto the plane. An upgraded theme park ticket will allow you to cut to the front of the line.

But if you have a few thousand dollars to make your trip to Disney World more enjoyable, why not use that money for something far more consequential? Like skipping the incredibly long line at your doctor's office.

'If you look at it from the perspective of people who have high income, or relatively high income,' says Asaf Bitton, a primary care doctor and associate professor of medicine and health care policy at Harvard Medical School and the T.H. Chan School of Public Health, '$2,000 or $5,000 a year is something you'd spend on a vacation. So I've heard people say: 'Why wouldn't I spend that on my health?''

Over the last two decades, that's exactly what has happened, as more and more doctors and patients have turned to 'concierge medicine,' which provides enhanced medical services for a yearly membership fee. Often, that fee falls between $2,000 to $10,000 a year, but it can run as high as $30,000 or $50,000, in addition to typical insurance reimbursements.

The fee generally gets you more time with your doctor, quick access to appointments, better connections with a network of specialists, and a simple way to contact your doctor when you need advice. But the rise of concierge medicine puts pressure on an alreadystrained primary care system - a system that's not attracting enough physicians and has become increasingly reliant on nurse practitioners, nurses, and physician assistants.

Why is concierge medicine growing? In large part, because dealing with health care is tough on both patients and providers, and lots of people want an alternative. 'The United States has not valued or financed primary care appropriately for decades,' says Erin Sullivan, an associate professor of health care administration at Suffolk's Sawyer Business School. 'The undervaluing of primary care - the under-financing of primary care - has led to a burned-out workforce and primary care staff shortages.'

Primary care doctors often see 2,000 or more patients a year, whereas doctors participating in concierge medicine generally see 800 or fewer. That allows those in concierge medicine to pay a lot more attention to each patient. 'You get access. You get time with your physician,' says Sullivan. 'The health system is a very complex place to navigate.'

For patients who can afford it, extra access is often worth the expense. By 2020, upward of 3 million Americans were already using concierge medicine, according to a report by NPR, The Robert Wood Johnson Foundation, and the Chan School of Public Health. Of Americans in the top 1 percent of income earners - defined by the report as households earning more than $500,000 a year - participation topped 20 percent. The size of the annual market for concierge medicine was nearly $7 billion in 2023, according to Grand View Research. Locally, Mass. General Hospital, Tufts Medical Center, and Brigham and Women's all run small concierge programs.

Michelle Domey's primary care doctor switched to concierge medicine about a dozen years ago, and she switched with him. 'At the time, I was having some medical issues,' she says, 'so it was important that I stayed with him. I thought it would be short term.'

Domey, who lives in Bellingham, says she never feels rushed during appointments. She never sits and waits. She can call her doctor almost anytime. And she can normally get specialists booked within 48 hours.

For Domey, the annual fee of between $2,000 and $3,000 per person is a sacrifice. 'We don't like it,' she notes. 'But we make it a priority. If you asked me to pay for this in another part of my life, it would be a hard sell.'

Though there's no central database of doctors who have elected to leave traditional medicine for a concierge practice - and that number includes specialists, as well as primary care physicians - Bitton says that he has seen an uptick in interest among his colleagues over time.

'The practice of primary care is incredibly stressful and feels to many like being on a hamster wheel,' Bitton says. Physicians are pushed to see a lot of patients, in order to bring enough money into the practice. And he says that they often stay up late at night dealing with electronic medical records and responding to thousands of messages a week.

'All that stuff leads a lot of people to say: 'Hey, I just want to be a doctor, and I want to be able to talk to my patients. I want to have a much more limited number of patients who I know and who trust me.''

Before he switched to concierge medicine in 2007, Jeffrey Bass took care of between 4,500 and 5,000 patients as a primary care doctor at Brigham. He would get to work at 4:30 or 5 a.m and often stay past 6 p.m. 'Eventually,' he says, 'I got to a point where it was unsustainable.'

Now, Bass - who has worked in primary care for nearly 40 years - sees about 450 patients in his Chestnut Hill practice. He still works fairly long hours, though not as long as before. But he says he likes really getting to know patients (physicals can run an hourand-a-half or more) and helping them live healthier lives. When Bass switched to concierge, he felt like he was doing something unusual; over the last couple of years, he says, patient demand has surged.

Some experts I talked to said that physicians who switch from traditional primary care to concierge medicine can double their salaries. And that matters: Part of the reason there's such a shortage of primary care physicians is that they typically make far less than surgeons, dermatologists, and other specialists. The Bureau of Labor statistics puts the average family doctor's earnings at about $240,000, while the average pediatric surgeon makes close to $450,000.

But the net effect of concierge medicine - which can lead to an improved quality of life for both patients and doctors - may have unpleasant ripple effects for society.

The pandemic caused a lot of primary care physicians to retire, says Alan Sager, a professor of health law, policy, and management at Boston University School of Public Health. Now, he notes, 'every time a primary care doctor leaves 2,500 patients and takes on 500 patients [in a concierge practice], we have 2,000 displaced patients, who are going to typically have a lot of trouble finding a doctor. ... The problem has been getting worse for a while, and it continues to get worse.'

Bitton also worries about growing inequities in primary care. He says that those most likely to succeed in concierge practices tend to be 'super productive, super experienced doctors' who would have served as advisers to younger doctors, nurse practitioners, and physician assistants in traditional primary care practices.

As Sager sees it, we simply need to change the incentive system. 'If we want more primary care [doctors], we have to pay them more.' He says that if we drop the average number of patients to 1,000 a year, it 'would give doctors time to think, have a relationship with the patient, visit the patient in the hospital, talk to the hospitalist. ... What we get for primary care is the foundation for medical security.'

For now, though, that foundation continues to crumble. And medical security is available to fewer and fewer patients.
