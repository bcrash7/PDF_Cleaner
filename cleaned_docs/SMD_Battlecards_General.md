## How to use this document

Each battlecard is structured for a sales rep working a physician prospect. The fields are:

- Snapshot — what the company is, current scale, ownership.
- Physician model — the contractual relationship the rep is selling.
- Pricing &amp; economics — what the physician pays the company and earns from patients.
- Strengths / weaknesses — framed as rep talking points and competitor attack lines.
- Voice of the field — quotes from physicians or industry sources, both positive and negative.
- Competitive plays — specific moves a rep should run vs. and from this competitor.

### Two structural facts the coach should never lose sight of

- **Affiliate vs. employer:** Five of the six (MDVIP, SignatureMD, Specialdocs, CCP, CCPHP) are **affiliation/management platforms** — the physician keeps ownership of the practice and pays a percentage of membership revenue. **PartnerMD is different:** physicians are W-2 employees of a Markel Group subsidiary. That changes every conversation.
- **Conversion model is the real product:** Full conversion (MDVIP, Specialdocs, CCPHP) maximizes income but forces the physician to release the majority of their patient panel. Hybrid/segmented (SignatureMD, CCP) keeps the panel intact and lowers risk but caps the upside. The right answer depends entirely on the physician's panel demographics, market, and risk tolerance — the rep's job is to surface that, not to push their flagship model.

## Current market context (2024–2026)

The U.S. concierge medicine market is in the middle of a multi-year acceleration. By volume of independent industry tracking, U.S. market value sits at roughly $7–8B as of 2024–2025, with consensus 10–11% CAGRs through the early 2030s (Grand View Research, Mordor Intelligence, Towards Healthcare). The number of U.S. concierge practices grew approximately 83% between 2018 and 2023; total practicing concierge clinicians grew approximately 78% over the same window.

Three structural drivers are shaping the affiliate-recruiting environment right now:

- Independent practice is shrinking. Per the AMA, only 42.2% of U.S. physicians remained in physician-owned practice in 2024 — down from 53.2% in 2012. Concierge affiliation is one of the few remaining off-ramps to retain ownership.
- Burnout is improving but still elevated. AMA Organizational Biopsy data (Oct 2025) shows physician burnout at 41.9% in 2025, down from 43.2% in 2024 and 48.2% in 2023. The exit-to-membership-medicine narrative has cooled slightly but remains the dominant emotional driver in recruiting calls.
- Specialty interest is real. Cardiology now represents the largest application share of concierge revenue (~27% in 2025 per Mordor Intelligence). Pediatrics is the fastest-growing application. The PCP-only era is over for the larger affiliate platforms.

*Every battlecard below should be read as the rep's answer to four physician anxieties: (1) will I lose my patients, (2) will I lose autonomy, (3) will I actually make more money, (4) what happens at retirement.*

#### Plays for the rep

**Selling against MDVIP:**

- Lead with take rate. ~30–33% royalty on every membership dollar, in perpetuity. Show side-by-side math at a $3,000 patient fee × 600 members: $1.8M gross, ~$540–$594K to MDVIP every year. Smaller competitors take less and offer more flexible structures.
- Specialty exclusion. If your prospect is a cardiologist, endocrinologist, pediatrician, or other subspecialist, MDVIP simply does not serve them. Push that aside fast.
- All-or-nothing conversion. SignatureMD's segmented and CCP’s hybrid models let the physician keep the legacy panel; MDVIP terminates anyone who doesn’t convert. For physicians with strong attachment to long-term patients, this is a real ethical and reputational concern.
- Use the PE-ownership narrative carefully. Goldman Sachs ownership doesn't bother every prospect, but for founder-led independent doctors it can resonate. Frame Specialdocs, CCP, and CCPHP as the founder-led alternatives.

**If you are MDVIP:**

- Anchor on scale and proof. 1,400+ physicians, 430,000+ members, NPS 89, 100 consecutive quarters of growth. No one else has these numbers.
- Lead with the patient-acquisition engine. Smaller competitors require the physician to do more of their own marketing and patient outreach. MDVIP runs a dedicated salesforce that fills physician panels.
- Succession planning is a real differentiator. After 25 years, MDVIP has helped hundreds of physicians retire — a story the founder-led competitors can’t fully match.
- Reframe the take rate as investment. The rep should be ready to itemize what the royalty actually buys: brand, MCE network, Travel Advantages, mental wellness program, conversion methodology, ongoing marketing.

#### Plays for the rep

**Selling against SignatureMD:**

- Attack the 8-year term. SignatureMD’s marketing is built on flexibility, but eight years is the longest initial term in the category. Specialdocs (no restrictive contracts) and CCPHP (joint-venture corporate structure) are cleaner.
- Probe the segmented model's labor cost. Hiring and supervising a physician extender adds W-2 staff, malpractice exposure, and clinical oversight load. Push prospects to model the all-in cost vs. a clean conversion.
- Question the undisclosed take rate. SignatureMD does not publish its percentage. Pricing transparency is a fair competitive question.

**If you are SignatureMD:**

- Lead every conversation with 'no patient abandonment.' This is the single most repeatable, emotion-anchored differentiator vs. MDVIP, Specialdocs, and CCPHP. Frame it as both ethics and reputational risk.
- Use specialty fit as a wedge. If the prospect is a cardiologist, endocrinologist, or pediatrician, MDVIP can’t serve them — SignatureMD does.
- Anchor on the six-figure investment per conversion as proof of alignment. The platform takes financial risk, not just operational risk.
- Highlight the 25% workload reduction with the doubling/tripling income claim — still the strongest economic narrative in the category.
- Use the rural hybrid product to open lower-density markets where MDVIP’s model is structurally incompatible.

## Universal physician objections (and how the coach should respond)

*These objections appear in every prospect call regardless of competitor. They are the most documented anxieties from physicians actually evaluating concierge transitions, refreshed against 2024–2025 industry reporting and peer-reviewed research.*

#### 'Will my patients follow me?'

Documented conversion rates from existing panels typically run 5–30%. A physician with 2,000 patients can expect 100–600 to convert depending on local affluence, panel demographics, and the strength of physician-patient relationships. Coach should: not promise a number; instead, walk the prospect through a panel survey and demographic analysis as the first step. Every legitimate platform offers this analysis at no cost (MDVIP, SignatureMD, Specialdocs, CCP, CCPHP all market it).

#### 'What if not enough patients enroll?'

Industry data: model 5–10% annual attrition into year-one financials; expect a ramp period. Practices that depend on immediate full enrollment fail. Coach should redirect from full-conversion to hybrid/segmented if the panel survey indicates conversion will fall below the breakeven threshold. CCP’s hybrid and SignatureMD’s segmented models exist precisely for this scenario.

#### 'Will my income actually go up?'

This is now answerable with current data. Real-world: SignatureMD claims average $400K+ income gains in segmented conversions. MDVIP cites 'more income' broadly without disclosing target ranges. Specialdocs published cases (e.g., Dr. Moultrie, who launched mid-career and 'filled to capacity quickly' in 2024) show successful early-career transitions. Coach should walk the prospect through a clean math model with their actual panel size, conversion %, fee level, and platform take rate — not rely on the company's marketing average.

#### 'What about my patients who can’t afford it?'

CBS News (June 2024) and KFF Health News have documented patient frustration with conversions — cited concerns about continuity of care, search costs, and equity of access. The strongest answer is: hybrid model (CCP) or segmented (SignatureMD), where non-paying patients stay with the practice. Full-conversion models (MDVIP, Specialdocs, CCPHP) address this through continuity-of-care plans that route departing patients to local PCPs.

#### 'What about retirement?'

MDVIP markets the most mature succession program after 25+ years. PartnerMD has zero practice-valuation upside (W-2 employees). Specialdocs is now actively running multi-month physician-to-physician handoffs as its earliest 2002–2005 cohort retires — published examples in 2024–2025 show acquiring physicians coming in for 'many months' working alongside the retiring owner. SignatureMD allows the physician to retain practice ownership and sell or transition independently. CCPHP’s JV structure has fewer documented exit precedents given the 2014 founding.

#### 'Is the model still growing or saturating?'

Refreshed answer for 2025–2026 prospect calls: the U.S. concierge market grew approximately 83% in practice count and 78% in clinician count between 2018 and 2023 (Mordor Intelligence). MDVIP added 100 consecutive quarters of growth through Q1 2026. Specialdocs delivered a record 26 conversions in 2024. The category is accelerating, not saturating, in primary care, cardiology, and pediatrics. Coach should use the current market sizing ($7–8B U.S., 10–11% CAGR) when this objection comes up.
